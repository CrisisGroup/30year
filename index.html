Crisis Group’s 30 Years on the Frontlines

1995 – 2025

1993–1994

On a flight out of wartorn Sarajevo, Mort Abramovich and Mark Malloch-Brown conceive of an organisation to serve as the world’s eyes and ears, warning of impending conflicts before they erupt.

A small team, including humanitarian Fred Cluny and former Congressman Steve Solar, begin to flesh out the idea and search for funding, following an initial donation from philanthropist George Soros.

1995

The International Crisis Group is founded and our three-pronged methodology – on the ground analysis, policy recommendations and high-level advocacy – is put into practice in Sierra Leone, ahead of crucial elections.

1996

Crisis Group establishes a field presence in the Balkans and our report Why The Bosnian Elections Must be Postponed sparks debate. The Board urges the UN Security Council to act preventatively in Burundi.

1997–1999

Crisis Group relocates headquarters to Brussels. Kosovo Spring warns of ethnic violence weeks before it explodes. Global reach grows with projects in Central Africa, Algeria, Cambodia and an advocacy office in Washington D.C.

2000

Former Australian Foreign Minister, Gareth Evans is appointed President and Crisis Group expands further, opening projects in West and Southern Africa, South East Asia and advocacy offices in New York and Paris.

In response to the 9/11 attacks, Crisis Group opens offices in Islamabad covering Pakistan and Afghanistan, and Amman covering Iran, Iraq, Israel-Palestine, Syria, Lebanon and the Gulf States.

2001

CrisisWatch is launched: a global conflict tracker covering developments in over 70 conflicts and crises every month. Crisis Group is the first major international organisation to sound the alarm on the atrocities in Darfur.

2003

Understanding Islamism becomes a major reference source on Sunni and Shite Islamic activism. Leaders at the UN World Summit unanimously adopt the “Responsibility to Protect” principle following a drive by Gareth Evans.

2005

Crisis Group opens a Beijing office, one of the first foreign policy NGOs to do so. Secures major policy shift in Pakistan, persuading international partners to withdraw support to the military dictatorship.

2007

Louise Arbour, former UN High Commissioner for Human Rights, joins as President. Crisis Group exposes the Sri Lankan government's failure to meet obligations regarding the treatment of arbitrarily detained Tamil civilians.

2009

Kosovo declares independence based on the plan designed by Martti Ahtisaari, former Crisis Group Chairman and later Nobel Peace Prize laureate, strongly reflecting our recommendations.

2008

“We should all be thankful that we have an organisation like Crisis Group that contributes to peace and security in helping to resolve conflicts, to ring the alarm bells of the international community, and to be a mirror for the conscience of the world.”

— Colin Powell, Former U.S. Secretary of State

2011

Crisis Group condemns the arrest of Board member Mohamed El Baradei during Egypt's Arab Spring uprising. Reports soon published on unrest in Egypt, Yemen, Bahrain, Tunisia, Libya, Syria and Jordan.

2014

Jean-Marie Guéhenno, former UN Under-Secretary-General for Peacekeeping Operations is appointed President. Crisis Group reverses perceptions that Afghan war is cooling, preventing the premature withdrawal of NATO funding for government forces.

“In my own experience, for the Colombian peace process, I used many of the reports and the research that Crisis Group had made in Colombia. But also after I left government, the first source of information has been the International Crisis Group.”

— President Juan Manuel Santos

2015

Crisis Group plays a vital role in shaping the Iran nuclear deal, with many proposals from our 2014 report, Solving the Nuclear Rubik’s Cube, incorporated into the final JCPOA accord.

2016

Crisis Group launches work on gender and conflict. Our recommendations on transitional justice become a foundation for the historic peace deal that ends decades of conflict in Colombia.

2017

U.S. program established, dedicated to analysing how U.S. foreign policy affects conflict dynamics.

2018

Robert Malley, former Chief of Policy and MENA Program Director, becomes President. Crisis Group advocates against Saudi-led offensive on Hodeida port, helping prevent a humanitarian disaster and escalation of Yemen’s war.

2019

New Future of Conflict program analyses intersection between conflict, economics, climate and technology. Crisis Group facilitates discreet meetings between chavistas and opposition at the height of Trump's “maximum pressure” campaign against Venezuela.

Comfort Ero, former Africa Program Director, appointed President. In December, Crisis Group warns of looming war in Ukraine in our report Responding to Russia’s New Military Buildup Near Ukraine.

2021

Ahead of Kenya's elections, Crisis Group advised stakeholders on lowering tensions, helping mitigate the risk of violence. Western governments ease Afghan sanctions in line with our recommendations, averting a worsening humanitarian crisis.

2022

Crisis Group helps secure a UN Security Council decision to permanently exempt humanitarian activities from sanctions targeting al-Qaeda and ISIS, protecting lifesaving aid for over 100 million people worldwide.
